Statement of Objectives

for: Future Sketches @ MIT Media Lab



The catalyst for my creative career was watching my grandpa running around his backyard with a yard waste bag over his head while I gleefully dodged him, and the wickets he had stuck in the ground. He was an expert at playing - the type of play where the rules are fluid and no one is quite sure who is winning, but it’s probably everyone. Watching my grandpa play was a masterclass in the creative act and has guided me in my own creative process: starting with a spark of fun, pulling that thread, and adding layers until something beautiful and interesting comes of it. This is reflected in my undergraduate thesis piece, “I Can’t Focus When You’re Watching Me” for the New Media program at Toronto Metropolitan University. I started with the tool — a drawing plotter, something I was excited to play with. From there, I discovered its strengths and quirks to see what I could create with it. Through this process of play, I grew to love the machine’s method of drawing, laboriously tracing lines that would take a laser printer only an instant. This became the basis of the finished piece, a nervous drawing machine that reacts to being observed by scribbling over its own work. I was initially attracted to the Future Sketches group because it values and supports this method of creation. It seems like a place that would support me in making discoveries about my own interests and in turn uncovering delightful ideas that might get buried under the rigidity of traditional research and development.

After attending the Future Sketches information session and hearing about the collaboration that happens in the group, I found myself drawn to the atmosphere of the Media Lab as a whole. In my undergraduate degree, I was part of a small New Media program whose greatest facility wasn’t the fancy fabrication lab, but the makerspace – a room with tables and chairs, and a 3D printer that never worked properly, but it was alive and electrifying! It was a place where a friend usually had the exact solution for your problem that led to a breakthrough, where inspiration was derived from the coexistence of others in a creative working space. Unfortunately, this space was restricted for the final year and a half of my time in the program. The realities of COVID-19 resulted in the University mandating a year's worth of independent exploration. I want to experience the joys of parallel work and play again, and I think Future Sketches is a place where I could find that. The resource at MIT that is most exciting to me is the people, and the Future Sketches group fosters a space for wonderful people who have playful and creative ideas.

Currently, I am interested in removing barriers to the creative process when working with technologies external to a computer. Adapting software and code to interface with the real world has been the largest barrier that I’ve encountered, and I have benefited greatly from the hard work of generous strangers online to overcome them. One tool I’d like to build is for LED pixel mapping onto 3D objects. This process when done solely in an Arduino IDE can be complex and inaccessible since designing light patterns for unusual surfaces can be difficult to create without vizualization. I’m interested in developing a tool that projects video content and generative patterns onto a virtual representation of a 3D object. This in turn, translates in real time to the physical object. I’ve accomplished a multi-step process of doing this in Touchdesigner for a project involving a glowing, interactive model of a brain with an internal hub of LEDs. A more intuitive, live updating tool could eliminate the barrier between ideas and reality.

The second tool that I’d be excited to create is one to help interface with obsolete pen plotters and dot matrix printers from the 80s. I love these old machines, partly because of the iconic noises that they make, but mostly because they are relatively simple to understand and have the potential to be used in an educational context. Engaging with past technologies has an element of whimsy to it! Unlike the sterility of modern technology, which can be daunting, there is character to these older machines. They are beautifully simple and endlessly fun. I think that one of the reasons 3D printers are so popular in Maker education is because just by watching them, you can start to understand how they work and what might be required to control them. However, the communication protocols used in old pen plotters are antiquated and difficult to translate. I would love to design tools (libraries and/or physical adapters) that would allow something like an Arduino to easily interface with a wide range of old tech in a similar way that the FastLED library brings easy functionality to a wide range of LED chips. The community of makers and hackers who repurpose these old machines is small. Everyone has their own process of reviving them and I would be excited to contribute to the effort to modernize beautiful and useful old tools.

Engaging with technology in a fun and untethered environment is the best way to imagine possibilities that may otherwise be stuck behind a wall of troubleshooting and debugging. The projects I have in mind could smooth out some common speed bumps that I’ve encountered within my creative process, and make it easier for others to find a more direct path from idea to reality.
